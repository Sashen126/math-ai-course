# Introduction Draft

Deep learning has achieved strong empirical performance across vision, language, and scientific modeling, but its success still rests on an incomplete theoretical understanding of optimization and generalization. In practice, model quality depends not only on architecture and data, but also on the interaction between optimizer dynamics, regularization, and objective geometry. This creates a central research tension: methods that optimize quickly do not always generalize best, and methods that generalize well may appear suboptimal under conventional training-loss metrics. The optimization-generalization interface is therefore a core methodological bottleneck for modern AI systems.

Recent literature highlights three converging themes. First, optimizer design strongly shapes learning trajectories and final model behavior. Adam popularized adaptive preconditioning and momentum in a computationally efficient form, enabling stable large-scale training and rapid iteration in nonstationary settings (Kingma and Ba, 2014). Yet subsequent work shows that adaptive methods can produce weaker test performance than SGD despite faster apparent optimization, suggesting that optimization speed and implicit bias are distinct axes (Wilson et al., 2017). Second, large-scale optimization research has clarified that practical training pipelines combine algorithmic structure with implementation-level heuristics, making real-world behavior a product of both theory and systems constraints (Bottou, Curtis, and Nocedal, 2018). Third, advances such as AdamW and sharpness-aware methods indicate that optimizer updates can be interpreted as regularization operators, not just search procedures (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019; Foret et al., 2021).

A critical insight from this body of work is that generalization-relevant properties emerge during training dynamics. Stability analyses of SGD provide one mechanism: update noise and step-size structure influence sensitivity to training examples, which in turn affects expected generalization error (Hardt, Recht, and Singer, 2016). In parallel, geometry-based arguments propose that flatter neighborhoods in parameter space correspond to better robustness under perturbation, motivating objectives that directly penalize local sharpness (Foret et al., 2021). While these perspectives are often framed as competing explanations, they may be complementary descriptions at different levels of abstraction: stability captures sensitivity over data perturbations, while sharpness captures sensitivity over parameter perturbations. A unified treatment could better explain when each metric predicts performance and when each fails.

Despite progress, key open questions remain. There is no consensus metric that reliably predicts downstream generalization across architectures, scales, and training regimes. Findings that hold in controlled benchmarks may degrade at foundation-model scale, where optimizer-state memory, mixed-precision effects, and data heterogeneity introduce additional confounders. Moreover, optimization choices interact with objective design and data curation in ways that are still poorly characterized, especially under distribution shift and weak supervision. These gaps limit our ability to select optimizers and regularization strategies on first principles.

This project positions itself at that interface. We focus on how optimizer structure and regularization jointly shape generalization in over-parameterized models, with emphasis on the practical trade-offs between adaptive and non-adaptive updates. The research goal is to move from descriptive comparisons toward actionable criteria: when should one prefer SGD-like dynamics, when should one adopt adaptive preconditioning, and when should sharpness-aware modifications be layered in? Addressing these questions has direct relevance for model reliability, training efficiency, and reproducibility. More broadly, it contributes to a mathematically grounded training science for AI, where optimization is treated as a mechanism for both fitting data and controlling inductive bias.
